The Science of Reaction Time — 3 Skills Whack-a-Mole Really Tests

Blog · 2026.07

Whack-a-mole is usually called a "reflex game." But playing it well actually engages at least three distinct cognitive abilities at once.

How fast are humans, anyway?

Simple visual reaction time for adults is commonly cited at around 200–250 milliseconds — the time for light to reach the eye, the brain to process it, and a command to reach your finger. Add "which hole?" and "should I hit it?" and reactions get slower. The finding that more choices mean slower responses has been studied for decades and is known as Hick's law. That's why watching nine holes is harder than it sounds.

Skill 1 — Selective attention: where to look

The biggest gap between beginners and experts is gaze. Beginners chase each mole with their eyes; experts park their gaze at the center and monitor everything with peripheral vision — the same skill developed by ball-sport athletes and experienced drivers.

Skill 2 — Inhibitory control: the skill of NOT hitting

The moment bombs exist, the game changes character. You must suppress the automatic "see it, hit it" response. Psychology calls tasks like this Go/No-Go tasks, a standard way to measure impulse control. In that split second when you freeze your finger above a bomb, you're performing the same kind of cognitive task used in research labs.

Skill 3 — Working memory: reading the flow

The combo system adds another load: tracking your current combo, how far Fever is, how much HP the boss has left — all updated in real time. That live maintenance of information is the job of working memory. Ever get shaky at combo 9? That's working memory under load.

Will games improve your reaction time?

Honestly: research is mixed on how much game training transfers to everyday reaction ability. Your skill at the specific task clearly improves, but broader transfer should be viewed cautiously. So treat whack-a-mole less as a brain-training device and more as a fun way to check your focus today. If today's record beats yesterday's — at this game, at least, you've genuinely improved.
